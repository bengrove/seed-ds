# Compliance

Source: Seed Copy Style Guide 2026 + Brand TOV 2025.

Regulatory and claim language for consumer health products. Read this before writing anything that references a health benefit, outcome, or mechanism. When in doubt, flag for legal review.

---

## Structure/function vs. disease claims

Seed products make **structure/function claims only**. Never disease claims.

### Allowed (structure/function)
Describe how a product supports a structure or function of the body.
- `Supports digestive health.`
- `Supports healthy regularity.`
- `Supports gut barrier integrity.`
- `Supports skin health.`
- `Supports daily energy.`

### Not allowed (disease claims)
Anything that implies a product diagnoses, treats, cures, mitigates, or prevents a disease.
- `Cures IBS.`
- `Treats SIBO.`
- `Prevents infection.`
- `Heals leaky gut.`
- `Eliminates bloating.` (softer: `Relieves occasional bloating.`)

### Test yourself
If the verb can be replaced with a medical intervention word (`cures`, `treats`, `diagnoses`, `prevents`), rewrite it.

---

## Required disclaimer

Any structure/function claim triggers the FDA disclaimer on the surface.

### Standard FDA disclaimer (verbatim)
> These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.

### Where it appears
- Any surface (page, email, ad, social asset) that contains a structure/function claim must include the disclaimer.
- Pair the disclaimer with an asterisk (`*`) on the first instance of the claim on the surface.

### Pattern
`[Claim with asterisk.]*`
Then, lower on the surface:
`*These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.`

### Example
> `Daily DS-01® seeds your gut with beneficial bacteria.*`
>
> `*These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.`

## Strain-specific claims

You can only claim a benefit that has **published human clinical data** supporting the specific strain in the Seed product.

### Rule
- Claim at the level of evidence. If only one strain is studied, claim at the strain level, not the full formula.
- Link to the citation, not a secondary article.

### Never
- Extrapolate benefits from studies of different strains of the same species.
- Translate animal-model findings into human benefits.
- Stack unrelated study citations to imply a broader claim.

## Citation requirements for claims

When copy includes a claim tied to research:
1. Inline citation with a superscript number right after the punctuation (see `mechanics.md`).
2. Full APA citation in the reference list at the end of the page or email.
3. Link to the primary source (journal article, government agency), not a secondary news summary.

### Citation location reminder
- Citations come **after** punctuation: `Science is cool.¹`
- Em dash exception: `Science is cool¹—especially biology.`

## Promotional claims

### T&Cs
- Promotions requiring terms and conditions carry a dagger (`†`).
- If a secondary promotion is on the same surface, use a double dagger (`‡`) for the second.
- Daggers are superscript, placed after the first instance of the callout.

### Example
> `25% Off Your First Order†`
> `Refer to Earn $25‡`
>
> `†Valid through [date]. Terms apply.`
> `‡Rewards credit to primary account holder only.`

### Promo language guardrails
- Lead with the product, not the discount: `DS-01® Now 25% Off†`, not `25% Off DS-01®†`.
- Specificity: `30% Off • Ends Today` beats `Limited time offer!`
- One exclamation per surface max.

## Off-limits claim patterns

Avoid these framings regardless of how they are worded.

### Medical language
- `clinically proven to treat`
- `doctor-recommended for [condition]`
- `eliminates [symptom]`
- `replaces [medication]`

### Absolute claims
- `guaranteed results`
- `100% effective`
- `works for everyone`

### Comparative disease claims
- `better than [drug/medication]`
- `the only product that cures…`

### Implied diagnosis
- `Feeling bloated? You might have SIBO.`
- `If you have IBS…`
- `Diagnose your symptoms.`

### Fear-based framing
Even when technically a structure/function claim, avoid framing that creates fear or implies harm from not using Seed.
- Not: `Without Seed, your gut is vulnerable.`
- Not: `Most supplements fail your microbiome.`

## Ingredient trademark handling

Claims about trademarked ingredients must match the trademark holder's approved claim language.

### By channel
- **Prospecting (Meta, Google, TV):** use familiar ingredient names (ashwagandha, nootropics derived from ginseng root, theacrine) to match audience literacy.
- **Owned and retail PDPs:** use both familiar and trademarked names where space allows. When constrained, default to familiar.

### Format
- Trademark symbols are superscript.
- First instance on a surface carries the symbol. Later mentions can drop it.

## Audience-specific compliance

### PDS-08™ (Pediatric)
- Specify age range: `for kids 3-18`.
- Claims must match pediatric safety data.
- No implied diagnosis of childhood conditions.

### VS-01™ (Vaginal)
- Structure/function claims tied to vaginal microbiome support.
- Avoid infection-specific language (yeast, BV).
- Reference approved claim set from legal.

### DM-02™ (Multivitamin)
- Nutrient content claims follow FDA daily-value rules.
- Use `100% daily value of [X] essential nutrients` rather than absolute nutrient claims.
- The approved category descriptor is **comprehensive nutrition**, not "complete nutrition."

## Agent workflow for claim-bearing copy

When generating copy that contains a health, efficacy, or outcome claim:

1. **Identify the claim.** Mark each claim-bearing sentence.
2. **Classify it.**
   - Structure/function? OK with asterisk + disclaimer.
   - Disease claim? Rewrite.
3. **Check the evidence level.**
   - Strain-specific human clinical data? Cite.
   - Species-level or mechanistic? Weaken the claim or remove.
4. **Add the disclaimer.** Any surface with a structure/function claim must show the FDA disclaimer.
5. **Flag for legal review.** In your response, call out which lines carry claims and what evidence you cited.

### Response format for flagging
> **Claim review needed:**
> - Line X: "[claim text]" — structure/function, sourced from [citation]. Disclaimer included.
> - Line Y: "[claim text]" — disease-adjacent, rewrote to remove.

## Trademark reminders for claim copy

Full trademark list lives in `vocabulary.md`. For compliance, the key rule:
- **Always superscript.**
- **First instance on the surface** carries the symbol.
- **Claims tied to a trademarked component** (ViaCap®, CereBoost®, TeaCrine®) must reference the trademark holder's approved claim language for that ingredient.

---

## When unsure

Default to the more conservative claim. Flag the uncertainty in your response. Do not invent studies, extrapolate, or paraphrase claims that only appear in brand assets without verification.
